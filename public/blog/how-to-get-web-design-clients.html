How to Get Web Design Clients: 10 Proven Strategies for 2025

✍️ Zenvylo Team📅 April 5, 2025⏱️ 11 min read

Web design is one of the most in-demand services in the world — but also one of the most competitive freelance markets. The designers making $8,000–$20,000/month aren't better than you. They just have a more systematic, targeted approach to client acquisition.

Here are 10 strategies that actually work in 2025. No generic advice about "building a portfolio" — these are specific, actionable methods you can start today.

1

Target businesses with visibly bad websites

The best web design prospects already know they have a problem — they just haven't gotten around to fixing it. Look for businesses with sites built before 2018, sites that aren't mobile-responsive, sites with no SSL ("Not Secure" warning), or sites that load in over 4 seconds. These are your warmest leads. They need you — you just need to show up and make the case.

2

Niche down by industry, not by skill

"I build websites" gets you $500 projects. "I build websites for dental practices" gets you $3,000 projects. Niching by industry lets you speak the client's language, show relevant case studies, and charge a premium for expertise. Pick one vertical you understand — healthcare, legal, fitness, food service — and go deep. You can always expand later.

3

Use AI to find and score local business leads

Manually researching local businesses with bad websites is slow. AI lead tools like Zenvylo can scan a city + service type and return 10 scored prospects in 2 minutes — complete with each business's specific website problems, a 0–100 opportunity score, and suggested monthly rate. This turns a half-day of research into a 5-minute task. Focus on leads scoring above 75.

4

Lead with PageSpeed as your opening hook

Google PageSpeed Insights gives you a free, objective, third-party audit of any website. If a business's site scores below 50/100, that's your opening line. "I ran your site through Google's speed test and it scores 32/100 — that means about 40% of people who click your site leave before it loads." This is specific, credible, and creates immediate urgency without you making any claims yourself.

5

Build an "unsolicited redesign" for your target niche

Take a real local business's website in your target niche and redesign it (clearly labeled as unsolicited). Show the before and after. Post it on LinkedIn, Behance, and Twitter/X with the business's city and industry tagged. You get a portfolio piece, industry authority, and occasionally the actual business reaches out to hire you. Takes one weekend — delivers leads for months.

6

Partner with non-competing local service providers

Find people who already have relationships with your ideal clients: business coaches, accountants, marketing consultants, print shops, photographers. Offer a referral fee (10–20% of the first project) for every client they send you. One good referral partner can send you 3–5 projects a year on autopilot. This is entirely passive once the relationship is set up.

7

Pitch maintenance retainers, not one-off projects

One-off website projects are unstable income. Offer a "Website Care Plan" — monthly hosting, updates, security monitoring, content changes — for $100–$400/month. Many businesses will say yes because they don't want to think about their website after launch. 10 care plan clients at $200/month = $2,000/month in passive recurring revenue while you keep doing project work.

8

Cold email with the "PageSpeed audit" hook

Run a prospect's website through PageSpeed Insights. Take a screenshot of the score. Email them: "I ran your site through Google's speed test — it's scoring [X]/100 which means you're likely losing [Y]% of visitors before the page loads. I'm a web designer in [city] who specializes in [niche] sites — I fixed this exact problem for a [similar business] last month. Worth a quick chat?" Specific, helpful, not salesy.

9

Attend local business events (in-person still works)

Chamber of commerce meetings, BNI groups, local business associations. One good referral from a local business relationship is worth more than 100 cold emails. You only need to find 2–3 "connector" types who know everyone in their industry. Bring business cards with your URL and one compelling case study printed on the back. Show up consistently — at least once a month.

10

Make follow-up systematic, not manual

Most web designers send one email and give up. The average deal closes after 5 touchpoints. Build a 5-email, 30-day follow-up sequence for every prospect you contact. Day 1: initial email. Day 4: follow-up with a useful tip. Day 10: case study from a similar client. Day 18: light urgency. Day 30: "last email" — creates a decision. Track everything in a CRM so nothing slips through.

What to charge as a web designer in 2025

Rates vary significantly by location, niche, and scope — but here's a realistic benchmark:

  • Small local business website (5 pages): $1,500–$4,000 one-time
  • Niche industry site (dental, legal, medical): $3,000–$8,000
  • E-commerce site: $5,000–$15,000+
  • Monthly maintenance / care plan: $100–$500/month
  • SEO + web design bundle: $600–$2,000/month retainer

💡 Rate tip: Never compete on price. If a prospect says you're too expensive, don't lower your rate — change the scope. "For that budget, I can do X pages instead of Y." Clients who start by negotiating your rate become your worst clients. The ones who don't haggle become your best ones.

The one thing most web designers skip

Consistent, proactive prospecting. Most freelancers wait for referrals. The ones making the most money are the ones running 5+ targeted outreach campaigns per week — finding businesses with problems, making specific offers, following up relentlessly.

AI tools have made this dramatically easier. What used to take a half day now takes 20 minutes. There's no excuse not to be doing it.
